# Model Card: `inventory` (Holt's linear smoothing + reorder-point formula)

Following the structure proposed by [Model Cards for Model Reporting](https://arxiv.org/abs/1810.03993) (Mitchell et al., 2019).

> **Status: demo-only, no real supply chain.** GlassCart has no real sales or stock data, so this forecasts a synthetic 90-day history (see [datasets/inventory/DATASET_CARD.md](../../datasets/inventory/DATASET_CARD.md)), not real demand. It's surfaced on a Glass Mode-gated `/inventory` ops page (see [docs/subsystems/inventory.md](../../docs/subsystems/inventory.md)), the same treatment as the pricing and fraud subsystems.

## Purpose

Demonstrate two classical, real inventory-management techniques end-to-end — exponential-smoothing demand forecasting and the reorder-point formula — including handling a real complication (censored demand on stockout days) rather than a clean, idealized signal.

## Architecture

- **Demand forecasting**: Holt's linear exponential smoothing (level + trend), applied to a weekly-deseasonalized version of each product's daily `units_sold` series. A per-product weekday seasonal index is computed first (each weekday's average sales relative to the overall mean), the series is deseasonalized by it, Holt's method estimates a smoothed level and trend, and the forecast for a future day reseasonalizes by that day's weekday index. `α = 0.3` (level) and `β = 0.1` (trend) are hand-set smoothing constants.
- **Stockout correction**: on a day flagged `stockout` in the dataset, `units_sold` is a *censored* undercount of true demand (stock ran out before demand was satisfied). Rather than feed that truncated number into the smoothing update, the model substitutes its own current level+trend estimate for that day — a standard, simple censored-demand correction (see [docs/research/inventory.md](../../docs/research/inventory.md)), not a sophisticated one, but a real one.
- **Reorder point (ROP)**: `forecasted_daily_demand × lead_time + safety_stock`, where `safety_stock = z × demand_std_dev × √lead_time` — the standard formula from classical inventory theory, with `z = 1.65` (~95% single-sided service level) and an **assumed** lead time of 7 days for every product (see "Known Limitations" for why this is assumed rather than measured).
- **Output per product**: current stock, average forecasted daily demand over a 14-day horizon, the computed reorder point, days of supply remaining, and a `needs_reorder` flag (`current_stock <= reorder_point`).

## Training Data

None — no training happens. `training/inventory/forecast.py` is a deterministic function (Holt's method is a fixed update rule, not something fit via optimization) evaluated over `datasets/inventory/inventory_history.json`.

## What GlassCart Does With It

`training/inventory/forecast.py` computes a forecast and reorder recommendation for every product and writes `models/inventory/inventory_forecasts.json` plus a `manifest.json` (methodology, constants, and how many products currently need reordering). `apps/web/src/pages/InventoryPage.tsx` — a small Glass Mode-only ops view, linked from the footer — renders the forecast table alongside a methodology panel.

## Metrics

No forecast-accuracy metric (e.g. MAPE) is reported against held-out future data — there is no real future to hold out, since the "future" here would just be more synthetic data generated by the same process being forecast, which would be circular in a different way than reviews'/fraud's benchmark caveats. What can be, and was, checked: `needs_reorder` fires exactly on the low-stock, high-recent-demand products expected from spot-checking the underlying series by hand (see the dataset's sawtooth stock pattern) — an internal-consistency sanity check, not an accuracy claim.

## Confidence & Uncertainty

The reorder point's safety-stock term is explicitly a function of demand variability (`demand_std_dev`), which is the one place this subsystem quantifies uncertainty — a higher-variance product gets a larger safety margin for the same average demand, exactly as the classical formula intends. The forecast itself is a point estimate (no prediction interval is computed or shown).

## Hardware Used

Pure Python, stdlib only — no GPU, no ML framework, negligible runtime (312 products' 90-day histories forecast in well under a second).

## Known Limitations & Failure Cases

- **Lead time is assumed (7 days for every product), not measured or inferred** — deliberately, since a real analysis has no legitimate way to know `datasets/inventory/generate.py`'s internal per-product lead time without reading simulation ground truth, which would defeat the point of demonstrating the technique on observable data. A real deployment would use each product's actual contracted supplier lead time.
- **Only 90 days of history** feeds the smoothing — too short to reliably separate trend from noise or observe more than one seasonal cycle (see the [dataset card](../../datasets/inventory/DATASET_CARD.md)).
- **Stockout correction is simple substitution**, not a principled censored-demand estimator (e.g. no explicit likelihood model for the true, unobserved demand) — a reasonable approximation, not the most rigorous available technique.
- **Smoothing constants (`α`, `β`) and the service level (`z = 1.65`) are hand-picked**, not tuned against any forecast-error objective.
- **No cross-product substitution or promotional effects** are modeled, matching the dataset's own limitations.

## Ethical & Privacy Considerations

- All sales, stock levels, and demand patterns are synthetic; no real supplier, warehouse, or sales data is processed anywhere in this pipeline.
- Reorder recommendations are display-only — nothing here would ever trigger a real purchase order, and the page is Glass Mode-gated specifically so a recommendation is never seen without the context that it's simulated.

## Intended Use

Demonstrating real, textbook inventory-management techniques (exponential smoothing, reorder-point calculation, censored-demand handling) end-to-end on a purpose-built synthetic time series.

## Non-Intended Use

Not intended for, and must not be used for, any real inventory or purchasing decision. Not validated against any real sales data or real forecasting benchmark.

## Reproducibility

```bash
uv sync
uv run datasets/products/generate.py       # if the product catalog changed
uv run datasets/inventory/generate.py      # regenerate the synthetic sales/stock history
uv run training/inventory/forecast.py      # rebuild forecast/reorder artifacts
uv run scripts/sync_web_data.py            # publish artifacts to apps/web/public/data
```

Re-running `forecast.py` against an unchanged history produces byte-identical output (Holt's method is a deterministic recurrence, no randomness).
